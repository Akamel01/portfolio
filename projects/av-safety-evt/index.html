Extreme Value Theory (EVT)

Why EVT for Safety? Standard statistics model averages (normal distribution), but safety is about predicting rare, severe events. EVT specifically models the tail of distributions using the Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) distribution.

GEV Distribution Block Maxima Tail Behavior

Bayesian Hierarchical Model

Three-level structure: (1) Data level‚ÄîGEV distribution for block maxima; (2) Process level‚Äîspatial random parameters capturing location-specific effects; (3) Prior level‚Äîweakly informative priors for robust inference.

Hierarchical Bayes Spatial Random Effects MCMC/Stan

Traffic Conflict Indicators

TTC (Time-to-Collision): Time until collision if trajectories continue unchanged. PET (Post-Encroachment Time): Temporal gap between vehicles at conflict zone. Extracted from AV sensor data to measure near-miss severity.

Why This Approach Matters

Proactive vs. Reactive Safety

‚ùå Traditional Crash-Based: Wait for crashes to occur, then analyze patterns. Reactive and limited by rare events.

‚úì Conflict-Based EVT: Analyze near-miss events in real-time. Proactive prediction before crashes happen.

Modeling the Tail, Not the Average

Safety isn't about average conditions‚Äîit's about the worst-case scenarios. EVT's GEV distribution specializes in modeling extreme tail behavior where crashes occur.

Spatial Random Parameters

Hierarchical structure captures location-specific safety patterns while borrowing strength across sites‚Äîmore robust than independent site models.

Model Transferability

Developed informed prior and recalibration approaches to transfer models across locations‚Äîaddressing data scarcity in new cities.
